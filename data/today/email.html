Day 31. The most expensive assumption in customer data is that an email address is a person. In every identity system I have worked on, the email-to-human mapping breaks in the same four ways. Shared family accounts. The work-versus-personal split. The dead address someone typed at checkout in 2019. And the person with eleven addresses who is, to your CDP, eleven strangers. Deterministic matching treats each of these as a solved key. It is not a key. It is a claim, and claims need corroboration: a phone that co-occurs, a shipping address that repeats, a device that keeps showing up at the same hour. When I built my open-source identity resolution engine, the first design decision was exactly this: no single identifier gets to assert personhood alone. Confidence is earned across signals. If your match rate report does not tell you how many of those matches rest on one lonely identifier, you do not have a match rate. You have a hope rate.
