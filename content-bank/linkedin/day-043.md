An honest note on failure: my first identity graph design at scale had a flaw I did not find for months, and the lesson changed how I build.

The design merged aggressively on normalized phone numbers. Reasonable, phones are strong keys. Except the dataset included call-center records where agents had entered the store's own phone number into the customer field, thousands of times, for walk-ins who declined to share one.

One phone number. Thousands of customers. The transitive closure did exactly what transitive closure does, and quietly assembled a mega-identity the size of a small town before anyone noticed downstream metrics drifting.

The fix was trivial: frequency caps on identifier reuse before merging. The lesson was not. Every identifier column contains operational garbage that looks structurally valid, and graph algorithms amplify garbage transitively.

Now every system I touch gets a hub-detection pass first. The data will always surprise you. The algorithm should not make the surprise bigger.
