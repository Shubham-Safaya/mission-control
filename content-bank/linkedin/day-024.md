There is a version of personalization that customers experience as service, and a version they experience as surveillance. The line between them is not creepiness. It is expectation.

A grocery app remembering my usual order is service, because the exchange is legible: I gave you my history, you save me time. A display ad that follows me across the open web with the shoes I browsed once is surveillance, because I never agreed to that relationship and no value came back.

The interesting part is the mechanism. Both experiences run on the same identity machinery. The difference is entirely in scope: first-party context versus everywhere, declared data versus inferred shadow, value returned versus value extracted.

This is why I am long retail media and short the surveillance-web model in the same thesis. One of these has a consent story that survives contact with the customer. The other one spent two decades hoping nobody would ask.

Customers asked. Regulators followed. Architecture is destiny.
