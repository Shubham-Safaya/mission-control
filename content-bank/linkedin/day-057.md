What building for the Indian diaspora taught me about identity assumptions baked into Western systems.

Name normalization libraries assume a first-last structure that large parts of the world simply do not use. Households in my systems assume nuclear structure; multigenerational homes with three adult generations under one roof confuse householding logic built for the American suburb. Transliteration means the same person exists as Shubham, Subham, and Shubh across records, defeating exact-match keys entirely.

None of these are exotic edge cases. They are the mainstream reality of billions of people and, increasingly, of Western customer files too.

The practical lesson for anyone building matchers: your normalization layer encodes a demographic worldview, chosen implicitly by whoever wrote it. Audit it against populations it was not written for, because your customer base already includes them.

Identity systems inherit the blind spots of their authors. Diverse test data is not politics. It is precision engineering.
