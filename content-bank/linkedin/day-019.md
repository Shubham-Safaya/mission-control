Findings from building a synthetic consumer population, which taught me more about real data than real data ever did.

I generated a hundred thousand synthetic people from public Census distributions to demo identity mechanics without touching a single real record. Age, household structure, geography, all statistically faithful, all fabricated.

The surprise was how quickly synthetic households exposed real algorithmic weaknesses. My own matching rules over-merged young adults living at the same address as their parents. They under-matched women whose surnames changed across records. Perfectly ordinary demographic patterns, invisible in production because production has no ground truth to grade against.

That is the argument for synthetic benchmarking in one sentence: you cannot compute precision without truth, and the only truth you can ethically own is truth you manufactured.

Every identity team should keep a synthetic population in the drawer. It is a flight simulator for the graph, and nobody gets hurt in a flight simulator.
