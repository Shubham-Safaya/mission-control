An underrated skill for data product managers: knowing which errors your system should prefer.

Identity resolution forces the question cleanly. False merge: two people become one, wrong order history exposed, wrong person deleted under a privacy request, measurement contaminated. False split: one person becomes two, duplicate emails, wasted impressions, understated LTV.

Neither is good. But they are not symmetric, and the right preference depends entirely on the consumer of the graph. Privacy operations should be terrified of false merges. Frequency capping mostly suffers from false splits. Measurement is poisoned by both but dies faster from merges.

So the threshold question, how similar is similar enough, has no single answer. Mature systems run one graph with multiple confidence cuts: conservative for anything privacy-adjacent, moderate for measurement, permissive for reach-oriented marketing.

When someone asks what threshold I recommend, my answer is a question: which mistake can you afford here. Everything else is implementation.
