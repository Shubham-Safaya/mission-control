Probabilistic matching has a reputation problem, and the industry earned it by using the word probabilistic to mean we would rather not say.

Actual probabilistic matching is respectable statistics: features, a model, calibrated scores, thresholds with known error rates. Fellegi and Sunter formalized it in 1969. It is older than the microprocessor.

What earned the side-eye was device-graph marketing that stretched probabilistic to cover co-located on the same coffee shop wifi. When precision numbers are unpublishable, the vocabulary gets creative.

The rehabilitation is straightforward and rare: publish calibration. A match scored 0.9 should be right about nine times in ten against truth data, and a vendor should show you that curve, not describe it.

When I hear probabilistic, I no longer hear a method. I hear a claim that requires a calibration plot. Bring the plot and we can talk. Adjectives are not evidence.
