Retail media's next margin fight will happen in an unglamorous place: the taxonomy.

Every network describes audiences in its own segment language: labels, definitions, hierarchies. Advertisers translating briefs across ten networks pay a hidden tax in mapping, and the mapping is lossy in ways that quietly reshape targeting. Health-conscious parents on one network is demonstrably not the same population as the identically-named segment next door.

Taxonomy sounds like housekeeping. It is actually market structure. Whoever's segment language becomes the industry's default becomes the reference against which everyone else is translated, and translation loss lands on the challengers.

This is why the standards conversations that look like committee tedium are worth attending, and why I pay attention when large buyers start publishing their own canonical taxonomies and demanding networks map to them. The direction of translation is the direction of power.

Vocabulary wars are real wars. In data products, the dictionary is the terrain.
