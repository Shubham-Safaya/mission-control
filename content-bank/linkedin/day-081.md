The graduation problem in data teams: systems built by seniors, operated by whoever is left.

The identity platform gets designed by the most experienced people in the room, encoding subtleties they understand deeply: why this threshold, why that blocking key, why the household rule has four exceptions. Then those people graduate, to bigger scopes, other companies, different problems. The system remains, operated by people who inherited the controls without the intuition.

This is not an argument against seniors building. It is an argument for designing systems whose subtleties are inspectable rather than remembered. Runbooks that state the why. Thresholds that carry their calibration history. Alerts whose response procedures assume no folklore. Simulation environments where a new operator can safely learn what turning each knob does.

The measure of an architecture is not how well it runs under its authors. It is how gracefully it tolerates their absence.

Build for your successor, arriving mid-incident, eighteen months from now, with questions.
