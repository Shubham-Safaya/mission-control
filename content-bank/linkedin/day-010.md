The dirtiest secret of identity matching is that most of the work is not matching. It is normalization.

Before any clever algorithm runs, someone has to decide that JOHN SMITH, john smith, and J. Smith Jr might deserve comparison. That +1 (555) 123-4567 and 5551234567 are one phone. That St and Street and STREET collapse. That the email with a plus-tag is the same inbox without it.

When I wrote my open-source engine, normalization ended up as more code than the matcher itself, and it deserved every line. Casing, unicode, nicknames, transliteration, address standardization. Boring, deterministic, endlessly consequential.

Bad normalization poisons everything downstream in both directions. Too aggressive and different people collide. Too timid and the same person fragments.

If you inherit an identity system and want to find its bodies, do not read the matching logic first. Read the normalizers. That is where the real decisions were made, usually silently, usually years ago.
