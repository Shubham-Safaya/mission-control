A defense of small data in an industry that only celebrates scale.

The most decision-dense dataset I work with regularly is not the billion-row event stream. It is the sampled human review queue: a few hundred matcher decisions a month, each examined by a person, each labeled correct or wrong with a note.

That tiny table calibrates everything. It converts match scores into believed precision. It catches normalizer regressions while they are anecdotes instead of incidents. It trains new team members on what ambiguity actually looks like, one household at a time. And it is the only dataset in the entire stack where we know the truth, because we manufactured it through judgment.

Scale tells you what the system did. Small, labeled, human-verified data tells you whether it was right. Confusing the two is how organizations end up with petabytes of confident error.

The review queue costs a few hours a week. I do not know a higher-leverage table in the building.
