The offline-to-online join is still the hardest problem in retail identity, a decade after everyone declared it solved.

The store transaction knows a card token and maybe a loyalty scan. The digital profile knows emails, devices, and behavior. Between them sits a join that decides whether omnichannel is a strategy or a slide.

Loyalty identifiers bridge it cleanly when the customer plays along. Payment-card tokens bridge some of the rest, with network rules and privacy constraints thick around them. What remains is probabilistic territory: timing, geography, basket similarity, and honestly, hope.

The metric I always ask for and rarely receive: what percentage of in-store transactions resolve to a known digital identity, split by method. That single number prices the omnichannel promise. Retailers with strong loyalty penetration live near the top. Everyone else is measuring their e-commerce site and calling it the customer.

The join is the business. Everything else is dashboards.
