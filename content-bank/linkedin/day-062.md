An argument for identity minimalism that I rarely hear inside the industry.

Every identifier you store is simultaneously an asset and a liability: matching power on one side, breach surface and compliance obligation on the other. The industry defaults to hoarding, on the theory that more signals mean better graphs. Past a threshold, the theory fails.

The marginal identifier adds noise faster than signal: another field to normalize, another source of operational garbage, another vector for over-merging, another row in the deletion workflow. Meanwhile its liability is constant and permanent.

The systems I admire keep spines deliberately thin: a small set of consented, high-quality identifiers, ruthlessly maintained, with everything else derived and disposable. Their match rates embarrass hoarders because quality compounds and noise does not.

Ask of every field in your identity schema: would we re-collect this today, under today's rules, for this use? If not, it is not an asset. It is inventory shrinkage waiting to be counted.
