The identity industry has a habit of renaming its hardest problem instead of solving it. The current name is interoperability.

Before that it was universal ID. Before that, cross-device. Before that, onboarding. Each generation of vocabulary describes the same underlying wish: that a person seen in one system could be recognized in another without loss, without leakage, and without anyone paying the toll twice.

The reason it never fully resolves is not technical. Matching across graphs is arithmetic. The reason is that identity translation is where market power lives. Whoever owns the join owns the margin, so every major player builds a bridge that is excellent at arriving and terrible at leaving.

I stopped evaluating identity partnerships on match quality alone. The question that predicts five-year outcomes is directional: how easily can I leave with my graph intact.

Interoperability is real when exit is cheap. Until then it is a pricing strategy with a standards body.
