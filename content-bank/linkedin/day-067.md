The pre-mortem I run before any identity architecture decision, five questions long.

One: when this merges two people who should be separate, who notices first, and how long until they can reach someone who can fix it? Two: when a regulator asks why these records were linked, can we answer from logs, or from folklore? Three: when the key vendor in this design raises prices forty percent at renewal, what is our exit, and how many quarters does it take? Four: when volume triples, which component quietly degrades before it loudly fails? Five: when the person who designed this leaves, what exists in writing?

No architecture survives all five cleanly. The point is knowing which failures you accepted, on purpose, with names attached.

Optimism is a fine culture and a terrible archivist. The pre-mortem takes one meeting and outlives everyone in the room.
