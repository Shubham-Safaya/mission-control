Why I maintain a personal error journal, and what a year of entries says about identity work.

Every time I am materially wrong about something professional, it gets a dated entry: the belief, the evidence I had, the correction. A year of identity-related entries has patterns worth sharing.

My most common error class: trusting an aggregate that had a composition problem underneath. Match rates that mixed strong and weak identifier matches. Coverage numbers that averaged strong and weak geographies. Aggregates flatter, compositions confess.

Second most common: assuming a data behavior was stable because it had been observed twice. Twice is an anecdote wearing a trend costume.

Rarest, but most expensive: believing a vendor claim because it was specific. Specificity reads as credibility, and it is the cheapest credibility to fake.

The journal costs five minutes per failure. It is the only performance review that never flatters, which is precisely its value.
