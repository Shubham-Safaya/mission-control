The quiet standardization war in retail media is not about ad formats. It is about who defines a person.

Every retail media network reports audiences and outcomes in its own identity dialect. An advertiser buying across ten networks receives ten incompatible definitions of reach, frequency, and household, which cannot be honestly summed. The industry bodies push measurement standards, and progress is real but slow, because comparability is expensive for whoever currently benefits from opacity.

Watch where the pressure comes from: the large agencies and CPGs, who bear the reconciliation cost, and the measurement intermediaries who monetize being the translator.

My prediction, held loosely: identity-level standards will not arrive by consensus. They will arrive when two or three networks with genuinely strong graphs realize that audited comparability is a sales weapon against everyone whose numbers cannot survive it.

Transparency tends to be adopted first by whoever it flatters. The good news is that in measurement, it flatters the honest.
